The Top 3 Costly Government Contracting Mistakes

Mistake #1: The "Generic Technical Proposal" Trap

What it is: Submitting a boilerplate technical proposal that fails to directly address the specific language, nuances, and unstated needs of the Request for Proposal (RFP) or Solicitation. Government reviewers can spot a generic submission a mile away, and it signals a lack of understanding or commitment to their unique problem.

Why it's costly: Immediate disqualification or a significantly lower technical score, pushing you out of contention before your price is even considered. Often, one proposal writer is tasked with both technical and pricing, leading to a diluted focus on technical specificity.

Insider Tip: Every RFP is a conversation. Read between the lines. Tailor every single section of your technical proposal to the agency's explicit and implied requirements. Use their exact terminology. Show you understand their problem better than anyone else.

Mistake #2: Pricing Precision & Contract Type Misunderstanding

What it is: Failing to price the solicitation exactly as required, or misunderstanding the implications of the contract type. Many government contracts are Firm-Fixed Price (F-F-P), which the government prefers as it places all risk on the contractor. Errors here include:

  • Inaccurate, inconsistent, or poorly substantiated cost proposals.
  • Lack of clear justification for proposed costs, especially for indirect rates.
  • Not accounting for the full risk of an F-F-P contract, leading to losses.
  • Misunderstanding the nuances of Cost-Reimbursement, Time & Materials (T&M), or Labor Hour (LH) contracts (which exclude materials).

Why it's costly: Pricing must be exact. Errors lead to immediate rejection for being “non-responsive,” or trigger lengthy, costly negotiations that erode your profit margins. Misjudging contract type risk can turn a winning bid into a financial disaster.

Insider Tip: Treat your cost proposal with the same rigor as your technical solution. Ensure absolute consistency across all financial documents. Understand the risk profile of the contract type before you bid. For F-F-P, assume all risk and price accordingly.

Mistake #3: Neglecting Compliance & Scope Creep Pitfalls

What it is: Overlooking or misinterpreting critical requirements detailed in the solicitation's compliance matrix, Section L (Instructions, Conditions, and Notices to Offerors), and Section M (Evaluation Factors for Award). This includes:

  • Failing to address every single “shall” statement.
  • Not providing required certifications or representations.
  • Missing specific page limits, font sizes, or file naming conventions.
  • For Personal Services Contracts, unclear boundaries lead to “other duties as assigned,” expanding scope without compensation.

Why it's costly: These are often pass/fail criteria. A single missed “shall” or formatting error can lead to outright rejection. Unmanaged scope creep breeds contempt and financial losses.

Insider Tip: Build a detailed compliance checklist from the RFP. Dissect it. Assign owners for each requirement. For personal services, define deliverables and scope precisely to avoid undefined work. The government will check compliance; you must manage scope.
